On the Keeping of a Sea Journal

By Eleanor A. Fairweather · 19 June 2026 · 7 min read

Whenever I find myself growing grim about the mouth, I take to the margins of an old ledger and there set down the weather. The practice is older than either of us; sailors kept their reckonings in ink that would not run, on paper that would not rot, and trusted the page to remember what the mind, salt-tired, could not. A journal is a kind of ballast — it steadies the day.

What follows is less an instruction than a confession: the small rituals by which a blank book becomes a companion, and how the hand learns to love the uncoated grain of a good cream sheet.

The Harbour at Dawn

There is an hour before the gulls when the water lies like poured pewter and the masts make their slow arithmetic against the sky. I write then, because the light is honest and the ink behaves. The first entry of any day ought to be plain — the temperature, the wind, the colour of the east — before the mind is tempted into ornament.

Keep the record as you would keep a watch: faithfully, and without embellishment. The flourish can wait for the fair copy.

First Light

A few practical observances, gathered over many crossings:

  • Rule the page the night before; a margin drawn in haste is a margin regretted.
  • Date everything. The sea erases days faster than you would credit.
  • Note the barometer first, the mood second.

The Turning Tide

When the tide turns it does so all at once, and the harbour exhales. The figures below are the ones I keep at the front of every journal, copied by hand each season.

A Note on Ink

Iron gall darkens as it dries, which is to say the page you write is never quite the page you keep.1 I have come to like this. A good carbon ink, by contrast, is faithful from the first stroke. For setting a recipe in a margin I prefer a small fixed block:

ink:
  base: carbon
  binder: gum arabic
  note: "darkens little; trust the first stroke"

Provisions & Marginalia

A journal, like a ship, runs on small disciplines. Below, the manifest I keep taped inside the cover — checked, struck through, checked again.

  • Spare nib, wrapped in oilcloth
  • Blotting card, two three
  • A second journal, untouched

And that is the whole of it. The rest the weather will teach you.

  1. Iron gall ink oxidises on contact with air, deepening from a pale grey to near-black over a day or two. ↩
